# **What is Flow?** #
{: style="text-align: center"}

Occurs in an environment separate from everyday reality.

When fully immersed, all sensory input not related to the task at hand is filtered out, and an individual may go many hours at a time without eating, taking a break, or even taking notice of bodily discomfort.

https://www.technologyreview.com/s/415041/new-measure-of-human-brain-processing-speed/ (MIT Technology Review) – The brain processes information at around 60 bits per second, depending on the activity.

Thus, with an activity demanding enough, or worthy of one’s full attention, complete immersion becomes a possibility.

The hardest part of achieving flow may be the requirement of having autonomy in its execution. Flow must be achieved subconsciously, and this often requires the individual in question to have at least a basic mastery of the process at hand.

Furthermore, an activity must give immediate feedback, be it through observation of short term goals, or by comparison to a set of pre-internalized standards.

A further difficulty in achieving flow is the balancing of level of skill and challenge. When skill exceeds challenge, a task becomes boring and does not elicit complete focus. When challenge exceeds skill, the task can become frustration and can induce anxiety. A task with a steep learning curve can seem overwhelming, but if tackled at the right rate can be the perfect host for flow.

According to Mihaly, the key to living a happy, fulfilled life is to live in The “autotelic individual” refers to a person with the ability to induce flow regularly, in a variety of occupations.

The term “autotelic”, from the English “auto“ meaning self and the Greek “telos” meaning end, refers to the property of something being entirely self-contained. In the context of flow, this means for an activity to be intrinsically rewarding – for an activity to be a goal in itself, not necessarily being part of a larger plan of action.
